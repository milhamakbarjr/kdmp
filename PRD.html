1.TL;DR

Our FMCG distribution business runs on phone calls, WhatsApp, and spreadsheets. We want to replace that with a single internal product. Before we spend engineering quarters on it, we need a working visual proof that the operator's day, the store's two minutes, and the driver's route all fit together.

kdmp v1 is that visual proof. It is a frontend mockup of five surfaces (DC Ops dashboard, Store mobile, Driver app, Regional Supervisor view, Executive KPI summary) wired to typed mock fixtures. Stakeholders can click through realistic data, walk through five scripted scenarios, and decide whether to fund the production build.

The product decision this mockup is built to test

Pull based ordering keeps the stock out problem. Pure auto allocation over ships. The suggested orders loop (system drafts, store approves) is the smallest design that removes "remember to order" without removing store agency. The mockup exists to make leadership feel that loop, not to argue for it on slides.

2.Problem and Context

2.1 Where we are today

We distribute fast moving consumer goods (ambient categories: snacks, beverages, toiletries) to modern trade stores across Java. Stores call when they notice they are running low. By then the shelf has been empty for a day, and the missed sale is permanent. Internally we operate from spreadsheets and group chats. There is no system of record for shipments, expected stock on hand, or today's truck assignments. Every DC works its own way; every supervisor has their own Excel.

2.2 Why a mockup first

  • Confidence before commitment. The full build is a multi quarter investment. We want leadership and ops to walk the workflow before signing the check.
  • Design first, not engineering first. Most of the risk in this product is workflow design, not technology. A mockup surfaces design problems while they are still cheap to fix.
  • Concrete artifact for ops review. A clickable mockup gets a sharper critique from DC managers than a slide deck or a Figma flow.

2.3 What this mockup is and is not

Is: a clickable web app with five surfaces, populated by realistic mock data, runnable locally, demoable in a meeting, capable of telling the story of a full operational day in fifteen minutes.

Is not: a production system, a connected app, a hosted SaaS, a backend, a real data pipeline, or a finished spec. Numbers shown are illustrative, not measured. Actions persist only within the session and only against in memory mock state.

3.Goals, Non Goals, Constraints

3.1 Goals

  1. Make the suggested orders loop felt, not described. A reviewer can click through the loop on each surface and recognise it as the right operating model.
  2. Show density without showing chaos. The DC Ops dashboard has to look like a real operator's tool: many rows, many filters, fast keyboard motion. Not a marketing screenshot.
  3. Demonstrate exception flow. When something goes wrong (driver flags closed store, store edits suggested qty, partial delivery), the mockup shows what happens and where the operator sees it.
  4. Be presentable on a laptop, in a browser, with no setup. One bun dev and the entire mockup is live.

3.2 Non Goals

  • Any backend service. No API, no database, no auth provider, no background jobs.
  • Real data, real customers, or real stores. All names, addresses, and SKUs are fictional.
  • Real fulfillment math. The "suggested orders engine" is a deterministic mock that returns plausible numbers, not a forecasting model.
  • User accounts and authorization. A role switcher in the header lets the demo viewer impersonate any persona instantly.
  • Returns, cold chain, hazardous goods, multi DC stock balancing.
  • Productization for external distributors.
  • Internationalization beyond fixed Indonesian formatting (currency, dates, sample text).

3.3 Constraints

  • Frontend only. The deliverable runs entirely client side. Mock data lives in src/mocks/. Network calls are forbidden.
  • shadcn/ui only. All UI is composed from src/components/ui/. No new component primitives without a PRD change.
  • Stack: TanStack Start, TanStack Router (file based), React 19, TypeScript 6, Tailwind CSS 4. See project CLAUDE.md.
  • Copy quality: active voice, specific over vague, no buzzwords, no em dashes, no exclamation points in product copy.
  • Geography and locale: Java, Indonesia. WIB timezone. IDR currency with period grouping (Rp 1.234.567). UI language English in v1, Bahasa Indonesia copy on field surfaces as a stretch goal.

4.Users and Personas

Five personas. In the mockup, a role switcher in the app header lets a single reviewer try every surface in one session.

Rina, DC Ops Manager Primary

Runs a distribution center. Lives in the dashboard.

Reviews the suggested orders queue, confirms dispatch, assigns trucks and drivers, watches in transit, handles exceptions. Optimises for throughput and on time rate. Lives on desktop.

Needs: dense tables, keyboard shortcuts, bulk actions, exception filters, low latency interactions.

Budi, Store Owner Data producer

Mobile only. Opens the app once or twice a day.

Receives a draft suggested order. Confirms or edits quantities, then submits. Confirms receipt when delivery arrives.

Needs: a simple list with suggested qty, one tap approve, big buttons.

Andi, Driver Field

Mobile only. In a truck during the day.

Sees today's route, marks each stop delivered, captures proof of delivery, reports exceptions.

Needs: large tap targets, minimal text input, stays one screen ahead.

Sari, Regional Supervisor Monitor

Web. Glances 1 to 3 times a day.

Owns a cluster of stores. Watches fulfillment health, drills into exceptions, escalates to DC Ops. Read only.

Needs: cluster filter, exception view, fast drill down to a specific order.

Pak Hadi, Head of Supply Chain Exec

Web. Daily glance, weekly deep dive.

Reads top line KPIs: stock out rate trend, on time delivery, suggested order acceptance, average cycle time.

Needs: clean summary, period selector, no operational actions.

System actor, Replenishment Engine Mock

Client side simulator in the mockup.

In the real product this is a server job. In the mockup it is a deterministic function over fixtures that produces suggested orders on demand, so demos always show the same shape of data.

5.Core Concept: the Suggested Orders Loop

The product hinges on one closed feedback loop. The mockup must let any reviewer trace the loop across every surface in under fifteen minutes.

  1. Infer. For each (store, SKU) the mock engine returns an estimated stock on hand based on last delivered qty and a default daily burn rate. In the real product this is a recurring job; in the mockup it is a memoised function.
  2. Suggest. When days of cover drops below threshold, the SKU joins that store's draft suggested order. Orders are batched per store on a cadence.
  3. Nudge. When the draft is ready, the Store surface shows a "ready to review" card.
  4. Approve. Budi opens the draft, accepts, edits, or removes lines, then submits.
  5. Confirm and dispatch. Rina sees the new order. She confirms it, assigns it to a wave with a truck and driver.
  6. Deliver. Andi sees the route, delivers, captures POD.
  7. Reconcile. Delivered qty becomes the new "last delivered" in the mock state, closing the loop for the next cycle.

How the loop runs in the mockup

The entire loop runs in memory. The mock state object is held in a Zustand or similar client store. Submitting an order updates the store state immediately; the next surface a reviewer visits sees the change. On page refresh, the mock state can either reset to a fresh demo baseline or persist via localStorage, configurable from the role switcher.

6.Primary User Journeys

6.1 Rina's daily loop

  1. Opens dashboard, lands on Today. Sees counts of orders pending approval, in pick, dispatched, in transit, exceptions.
  2. Opens Suggested Orders. Reviews submitted orders. Bulk confirms most; flags a few for manual review.
  3. Opens Dispatch. Groups confirmed orders into waves, assigns trucks and drivers.
  4. Watches In Transit. Acts on exceptions as they arrive.
  5. End of day: opens Reconciliation, confirms everything delivered, rolls over what was not.

6.2 Budi's two minutes

  1. Sees "Suggested order ready, 14 items" on home.
  2. Opens the draft, taps Approve all, submits.
  3. Later: delivery arrives, signs and photos receipt.

6.3 Andi's route

  1. Logs in, sees the day's route as an ordered list.
  2. Taps stop, sees the order, taps navigate.
  3. At the store, confirms qty delivered, captures photo and signature.
  4. Marks complete, scrolls to next stop.
  5. Reports exceptions inline with a reason code and a photo.

8.Mock Data Strategy

Mock data is not an afterthought, it is the product. The reviewer's belief in the workflow depends on the data feeling real.

8.1 Principles

  • Plausible, not random. Names, addresses, SKUs, and quantities should pass a glance test for someone who works in FMCG Java. Use Indonesian store names, Indonesian street addresses, common FMCG SKUs and brand archetypes (without using real brand names).
  • Deterministic by default. The same demo state produces the same screens every time. Stakeholders comparing notes should see the same numbers.
  • Scenario seeded. The role switcher exposes a "scenario" selector that loads a different mock state (calm Tuesday, exception heavy Friday, end of month surge). This is how we tell the same story with different stress levels.
  • Action mutates state. When a reviewer confirms an order, the state updates and other surfaces reflect the change. Refresh resets to the scenario baseline unless persistence is toggled on.

8.2 File layout

PathContents

8.3 Loading and error states

Every surface shows realistic loading and error states, simulated by client side timers and a "simulate error" toggle in the role switcher. The mockup must look correct in each of: loading, empty (no orders today), populated, error. Skeletons use Skeleton; empty states are compositions of Card with an icon, a one line message, and a primary action.

10.Frontend Quality Bar

Backend NFRs (availability, throughput, durability) are not applicable. The bar below applies only to the frontend deliverable.

10.1 Performance

  • Initial paint of the DC Ops dashboard under 1.5 seconds on a wired desktop.
  • Queue interactions under 200 ms perceived latency. Long lists virtualise if they cross 200 rows.
  • The In Transit timer must not block the main thread.

10.2 Responsive behavior

  • DC Ops dashboard is desktop first, usable down to 1280 px wide. Below that, show a "switch to desktop" notice rather than degrade.
  • Store and Driver surfaces are mobile first, work down to 360 px wide.
  • Supervisor and Exec surfaces are usable on both desktop and tablet.

10.3 Accessibility

  • WCAG AA on the DC Ops dashboard. Color contrast verified for every Badge status variant.
  • All shadcn components used in their unmodified form; their built in keyboard and ARIA behavior must not be overridden.
  • Mobile surfaces: minimum 44 by 44 pt tap targets, focus rings preserved.

10.4 Visual quality (per /impeccable)

  • Color in OKLCH (already configured in src/styles.css). No pure black, no pure white in product surfaces.
  • Typography hierarchy via scale and weight contrast, at least 1.25 ratio between steps.
  • No side stripe borders, no gradient text, no glassmorphism by default, no hero metric template, no identical card grids, no modal as first thought. Banned by the design system; banned here too.
  • Spacing varies for rhythm. Same padding everywhere is monotony.

10.5 Copy quality (per /marketing-skills:copywriting)

  • Active voice. Specific over vague. Customer language over company language.
  • No em dashes. No exclamation points in product copy. No marketing buzzwords.
  • CTAs say what the user gets: "Confirm orders", "Submit to DC", "Mark delivered". Never "Submit", "OK", "Click here".
  • Empty state copy describes what to do next, not what is absent.

11.Demo Scenarios and Success Criteria

Because there is no live data, success is judged by how well the mockup tells the story in a stakeholder review. Five scripted scenarios cover the daily loop and the most important edge cases.

ScenarioWhat the reviewer doesWhat proves it works
1. Calm Tuesday Switch to Rina. Review 12 suggested orders. Bulk confirm. Build one dispatch wave. Switch to Andi, complete a route. The reviewer never asks "what do I do here?". Each surface presents the single obvious next action.
2. Store edits a suggestion Switch to Budi. Open the draft. Reduce one line, remove another, add a manual note. Submit. Switch back to Rina. The order arrives in the queue with an "edited by store" indicator. Rina's view of the line shows requested vs suggested.
3. Exception during delivery Switch to Andi. Open a stop. Tap exception. Reason: store closed. Submit with a photo. Switch back to Rina. The exception is at the top of Today. The HoverCard shows the photo. Reassign or reschedule action works against mock state.
4. Cluster health Switch to Sari. Pick a cluster. Spot a store with three recent exceptions. Drill into one. Sari's surface has no write affordances. The drill down is a Sheet that opens fast and shows the relevant order.
5. Exec glance Switch to Pak Hadi. Read four KPIs. Switch period from Today to 30 days. Numbers and deltas change. The page is calm and uncluttered. No clickable affordances that lead nowhere.

11.1 What "done" means for v1

  1. All five scenarios run end to end without dev intervention.
  2. Every P0 requirement is implemented and visually polished.
  3. The mockup loads on a clean machine with bun install and bun dev, no environment configuration required.
  4. The reviewer's qualitative response to scenario 1 is "I would use this", not "I can imagine using this".

13.Risks and Mitigations

RiskLikelihoodImpactMitigation
Mock data feels too sparse or too convenient, undermining the demo's credibility High High Treat the fixtures as a design artifact. Review them with ops before S2 ships. Borrow real store name patterns and SKU archetypes from public sources.
Stakeholders mistake the mockup for a working system and ask "when can we use it?" Medium Medium The persistent banner at the top of every surface reads "Mockup: data is illustrative". The role switcher is visibly developer flavored, not a product affordance.
Scope creep into backend work ("can you just hook up a real database for the demo?") High High Explicit non goal in §3.2. Any request to integrate is a PRD change with PM sign off. Mock state already supports persistence via localStorage for multi session demos.
Designers and engineers reach for a custom component when shadcn primitives feel limiting High Medium Hard rule in CLAUDE.md. Composition first, rescope second, custom never. Charts and the signature canvas are the only acknowledged primitives outside the catalog (see §15).
Demo runs locally but breaks on a stakeholder's machine Medium Medium Single command setup. CI build on every PR. Optional static export via TanStack Start so the mockup can be served from any static host.
The mockup hides UX problems that only show up at production scale (1000 rows in a queue, 50 waves on a board) Medium Medium Include one "stress" scenario at upper bound row counts. Virtualisation is in scope for the queue if needed.

14.Open Questions

  1. UI language. English everywhere, or Bahasa Indonesia on the Store and Driver surfaces? Bahasa on field surfaces would make demos to ops more visceral.
  2. Mock state persistence default. Reset on refresh (clean every time) or persist via localStorage (continuity across a demo session)? Both are easy; pick one as default.
  3. Scenario count. Five named scenarios in §11. Do we need more (e.g. "warehouse short stocked", "new store onboarding")?
  4. Signature capture. Acceptable to use HTML canvas inside a shadcn Sheet for the driver signature? The canvas is a browser primitive, not a custom React component.
  5. Hosting for review. Static export to a private URL (Vercel, Cloudflare Pages) for asynchronous review, or local only? Static is recommended; needs sign off on the "demo could be screenshotted and shared externally" risk.
  6. Naming. The codebase is kdmp. Is that the product name in the demo or a placeholder? Product copy across the mockup depends on this.
